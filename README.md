1. Introduction
Global Retail Pro's management requires an accurate and objective assessment of the company's performance to support strategic decision-making in areas such as inventory management, marketing, and regional operations.
This report examines the company's sales transaction data collected over a four-year period, from 2011 to 2014. The dataset consists of 51,290 sales transactions from 13 different regions and includes information on three product categories. Analysing this data provides valuable insights into the company's sales performance, profitability, and regional trends.
The analysis began by cleaning, verifying, and organizing the raw data to ensure its accuracy and reliability. The processed data was then summarized into three key tables: Monthly Performance, Product Sub-Category Performance, and Regional Performance. These summary tables served as the basis for further analysis and visualization.
To better understand the data, three different charts were created: a scatter plot, a time series plot, and a bar chart. The scatter plot was used to examine the relationship between discounts and profits, helping to determine whether larger discounts affected profitability. The time series plot illustrates changes in sales and profits over the four-year period, making it easier to identify trends and seasonal patterns. Finally, the bar chart compares the performance of the 13 regions based on total profit and the number of orders placed.
The findings from these analyses provide meaningful insights into the company's overall performance and can assist management in making informed decisions that improve operational efficiency, increase profitability, and support future business growth.
2.0 Summary of Visualisations & Insights
2.1Scatter Plot: Profitability vs. Discount Strategy
 
Figure 1. Average discount vs. profit margin by product sub-category (bubble size = total sales, colour = category).
2.1.1 Analysis and Business Implications
The analysis reveals a strong negative relationship between the average discount offered and the profit margin across product sub-categories, with a correlation coefficient of approximately −0.88. This indicates that as the average discount increases, the profit margin tends to decrease. In other words, offering larger discounts generally reduces the profitability of a product category.
Among all the sub-categories, Tables stands out as the poorest-performing product. It has the highest average discount of 29.1% and is the only sub-category that recorded a negative profit margin (−8.5%). Although Tables generated approximately $757,000 in sales, the heavy discounting resulted in an overall loss of about $64,000. This suggests that the current pricing strategy is increasing sales volume at the expense of profitability.
On the other hand, sub-categories such as Paper, Labels, and Copiers performed much better. These products achieved profit margins of 24.2%, 20.4%, and 17.1%, respectively, while maintaining relatively low average discounts of between 11% and 12%. Their performance demonstrates that it is possible to achieve strong sales and healthy profits without relying on excessive price reductions.
These findings suggest that the company's current discount strategy for the Furniture category, particularly for Tables, should be reviewed. Reducing or limiting discounts on these products could help improve profitability while maintaining competitive sales. At the same time, the pricing strategies used for higher-performing sub-categories such as Paper and Copiers could serve as a useful benchmark for future pricing and promotional decisions across the business.
3.0 Time Series Plot: Sales Performance Over Time
 
Figure 2. Monthly total sales (left axis) and total profit (right axis), Jan 2011 – Dec 2014.

3.1 Analysis and Business Implications
The time series analysis shows that both sales and profit increased steadily over the four-year period. Annual sales rose from approximately $2.26 million in 2011 to about $4.30 million in 2014. Similarly, annual profit increased from around $249,000 to approximately $504,000 during the same period. This overall growth suggests that the company expanded its sales performance and improved profitability over time.
A clear seasonal pattern is also evident in the data. Sales are generally lower during the first few months of each year, particularly between January and April, before gradually increasing throughout the year. Performance reaches its highest levels between September and December, with November 2014 recording the highest monthly sales in the dataset at approximately $555,000. This recurring pattern indicates that customer demand is strongest during the final quarter of the year.
Although sales and profit tend to increase together, profit does not always rise at the same rate as sales. In several months with exceptionally high sales, particularly during November and December, the increase in profit is relatively smaller. This suggests that higher sales volumes during peak periods may be supported by larger discounts or promotional offers, which reduce the overall profit margin.
These findings have important implications for business planning. Since demand consistently increases during the final quarter of the year, management should prepare by increasing inventory levels and ensuring sufficient staffing before the peak season begins. At the same time, promotional activities and discount strategies should be carefully managed to ensure that higher sales translate into stronger profits rather than reduced profit margins.
4.0 Bar Chart: Regional Performance Comparison
 
Figure 3. Total profit and number of orders by region. 
4.1Analysis and Business Implications
The regional performance analysis shows that the Central region is the company's strongest performer. It generated approximately $311,000 in profit from 5,249 orders, making it the highest-performing region in terms of both total profit and order volume. This suggests that the region is not only attracting a large number of customers but is also converting those sales into strong financial returns.
Overall, there is a positive relationship between the number of orders and regional profit, with a correlation coefficient of approximately 0.74. However, this relationship is not consistent across all regions. For example, the South region recorded 3,270 orders, which is about 39% more than the North region's 2,356 orders. Despite this higher sales volume, South generated only about $140,000 in profit, compared with approximately $195,000 in the North. This indicates that the North region earns more profit from each order, suggesting greater operational efficiency or stronger product margins.
The analysis also highlights EMEA and Africa as relatively low-performing regions. Although EMEA processed 2,462 orders, it generated only about $44,000 in profit. Similarly, Africa recorded 2,232 orders but earned only around $89,000 in profit. In comparison, North Asia achieved approximately $166,000 in profit from just 1,150 orders, demonstrating much higher profitability per order.
These findings indicate that a higher number of orders does not automatically lead to higher profits. While the Central and North Asia regions provide examples of strong profitability and efficient performance, regions such as South, EMEA, and Africa appear to generate relatively low returns despite handling a significant number of orders. Management should therefore investigate factors such as product mix, pricing strategies, discount policies, and operating costs in these regions to identify opportunities for improving profit margins and overall business performance.
5.0 Integrated Data-Driven Recommendations
Based on the findings from the three visualisations, two key recommendations can be made to help Global Retail Pro improve its overall business performance and profitability.
5.1Recommendation 1: Review the Discount Strategy for Furniture Tables
The analysis indicates that the Tables sub-category requires immediate attention. The scatter plot shows that Tables receive the highest average discount (29.1%) while also recording the only negative profit margin (−8.5%), resulting in an estimated loss of approximately $64,000. This suggests that the current discount strategy is increasing sales volume but reducing overall profitability.
The regional analysis also shows that some regions, particularly South and EMEA, generate relatively low profits despite processing a large number of orders. This may indicate that heavily discounted products, such as Tables, are contributing to weaker financial performance in these regions.
To improve profitability, management should consider reducing the maximum discount offered on Tables to around 15% or a similar level used for more profitable furniture products such as Bookcases and Chairs. In addition, discount policies should be reviewed on a regional basis rather than applying the same pricing strategy across all markets. This approach would allow the company to respond to differences in customer demand and regional profitability.
5.2 Recommendation 2: Plan for Peak-Season Demand While Protecting Profit Margins
The time series analysis shows a consistent increase in sales between September and December each year, with November 2014 recording the highest monthly sales during the four-year period. This predictable seasonal trend provides management with an opportunity to prepare more effectively for periods of high customer demand.
To take advantage of this pattern, the company should ensure that adequate inventory, staffing, and marketing activities are in place before the start of the peak season. At the same time, promotional discounts should be carefully controlled, particularly for product categories that already achieve healthy profit margins, such as Technology and Office Supplies. While discounts can help increase sales, excessive price reductions during high-demand periods may reduce overall profitability. A balanced promotional strategy would allow the company to maximise both sales and profit during the busiest months of the year.
